Things I think about

Long-form reflections on AI safety, field-building, research, and where I'm headed. This page will grow over time — for now, start with the career narrative below.

A working theory of how I want to contribute

This is a living document. I update it as my thinking evolves. Last updated March 2026.

I'm finishing my undergraduate degree with an unusual amount of clarity about what I think matters and an unusual amount of uncertainty about what the right role for me is. What I know: I want to work on making AI go well. What I'm still figuring out: exactly where in the ecosystem I can be most useful.

What drives me

I got into AI safety not through a technical rabbit hole but through a question about responsibility. If we're building systems that will reshape institutions, labour markets, and the information environment — who is making sure that goes well, and are they asking the right questions?

The more I've learned, the more I think the field's biggest bottleneck isn't ideas. It's infrastructure: the organisations, processes, and talent pipelines that turn good thinking into good outcomes. That's why I founded AI Safety Saarland, why I now co-lead SAIGE's research incubator, and why I care as much about designing a good selection rubric as I do about training a good linear probe.

What concerns me

The gap between the pace of capability development and the pace of safety infrastructure is what keeps me up at night. But I'm equally concerned about the narrowness of who gets to shape these decisions — geographically, linguistically, disciplinarily.

My research on multilingual sycophancy isn't just a technical exercise; it's a probe into whether safety evaluations actually work for the majority of the world's population, who don't interact with AI in English. My work on context-aware evaluation asks whether we're measuring the right thing when we evaluate "safety" without knowing anything about the person on the other end.

I wrote a paper about this: context-aware evaluation reveals safety gaps of ~1.8–2.5 points (on a 7-point scale) for high-vulnerability users in finance and health. That's not a rounding error — it's a structural blind spot.

Where I think I fit

I've spent the last two years doing research, building organisations, and translating between technical and non-technical communities. The pattern I notice in my own work: I'm most energised — and most effective — when I'm sitting between research and operations.

Designing an incubator selection rubric. Structuring a research program. Figuring out how to evaluate something that doesn't have an obvious metric yet. Giving a talk about AI at a church youth group and then going home to debug a SLURM job. That range isn't a lack of focus — it's the thing I'm good at.

The profile I'm building toward: roles that require deep technical understanding but aren't pure research — strategy, research management, program design, or policy work close to the technical frontier. Places where understanding what a linear probe tells you about cross-lingual sycophancy and understanding how to build a cohort of 35 researchers across 17 projects are both valued.

What I'm looking for

Concretely, I'm interested in roles at AI safety organisations, labs, or policy institutes where I can contribute to strategy, program design, or research operations. I want to be in a place where the work matters, where I'll learn from people who've been doing this longer than I have, and where my combination of research experience and organisational instincts is an asset rather than an awkward straddle.

If you're hiring for something that sounds like this, or if you just want to talk about any of the above — I'd welcome the conversation.
